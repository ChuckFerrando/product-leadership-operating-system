# Outcome Intervention Playbook

The **Outcome Intervention Playbook** defines the canonical approach for translating evaluated outcome gaps into structured intervention actions within the **Product Leadership Operating System (PLOS)**.

Where the **Outcome Evaluation Model** determines whether outcomes have been achieved, and the **Gap & Intervention Model** frames gaps and intervention needs, this playbook defines how those intervention signals are operationalized without violating system boundaries.

It ensures that outcome-driven interventions are **disciplined, structured, and system-aligned**, rather than reactive, anecdotal, or execution-biased.

---

# Purpose

The purpose of this playbook is to provide a **repeatable, system-aligned method** for responding to outcome gaps.

It exists to:

- convert evaluated outcome gaps into actionable intervention paths  
- ensure interventions are grounded in validated learning, not raw signals  
- preserve separation between **evaluation (Outcomes)** and **execution (Delivery)**  
- ensure interventions properly inform **strategy, delivery, and Decision Intelligence**  
- improve the consistency and effectiveness of outcome-driven response  

Without this playbook, organizations tend to respond to outcomes in inconsistent ways, often jumping directly to execution changes without understanding root causes or misrouting interventions to the wrong system.

---

# Core Principle

> **Intervention follows evaluation — it does not replace it**

All intervention actions must originate from:

- interpreted signals  
- qualified value  
- evaluated outcomes  

Interventions must never be based on:

- raw metrics  
- isolated signals  
- anecdotal observations  

---

# Intervention Categories

All outcome-driven interventions must be routed into one of three system-aligned categories:

## 1. Strategy Intervention

Used when the gap indicates an issue with:

- strategic intent  
- outcome definition  
- value hypotheses  
- strategic assumptions  
- expected conditions or timelines  

### Examples

- refining target outcomes  
- adjusting value expectations  
- revisiting problem framing  
- modifying strategic direction  

### Ownership

- **Strategy Execution System**

---

## 2. Delivery Intervention

Used when the gap indicates an issue with:

- execution quality  
- feature design or implementation  
- release effectiveness  
- adoption enablement  
- operational performance  

### Examples

- improving usability or experience  
- correcting implementation issues  
- adjusting rollout approach  
- strengthening onboarding or activation  

### Ownership

- **Product Delivery System**

---

## 3. Measurement Intervention

Used when the gap indicates an issue with:

- signal quality  
- measurement accuracy  
- instrumentation coverage  
- metric definition  
- visibility or reporting  

### Examples

- improving telemetry  
- redefining metrics  
- enhancing dashboards  
- correcting data inconsistencies  

### Ownership

- **Decision Intelligence System**

---

# Intervention Decision Framework

Intervention routing must follow a structured decision process:

## Step 1 — Confirm Evaluation Validity

- Was the outcome formally evaluated?  
- Is the judgment supported by interpreted signals and value qualification?  
- Is confidence level sufficient?

If not, return to evaluation before intervening.

---

## Step 2 — Identify Gap Type

Determine whether the gap is primarily:

- **Strategic** → wrong problem, outcome, or value assumption  
- **Delivery** → delivery did not produce intended effect   
- **Measurement** → signals do not accurately represent reality  

---

## Step 3 — Validate Root Cause Hypothesis

- What is the most likely explanation for the gap based on evaluated evidence?   
- What evidence supports this hypothesis?  
- What alternative explanations exist?

Avoid premature intervention without a clear hypothesis.

---

## Step 4 — Select Intervention Category

Route the intervention to:

- Strategy  
- Delivery  
- Decision Intelligence  

Do not split ownership unless explicitly required.

---

## Step 5 — Define Intervention Scope

- What specifically needs to change?  
- What is the expected impact of the intervention?  
- How will success be evaluated?

---

## Step 6 — Route to Owning System

- Provide structured input to the owning system  
- Do not execute intervention within the Outcomes system  

---

# Operating Logic

The playbook follows this canonical sequence:

1. **Outcome evaluation produces judgment**  
2. **Gap & Intervention Model identifies gap**  
3. **Root cause hypothesis is formed**  
4. **Intervention category is selected**  
5. **Intervention is defined and scoped**  
6. **Intervention is routed to owning system**  
7. **Delivery executes (if applicable)**  
8. **New signals are generated and re-enter the system**

This preserves the PLOS loop:

**Strategy → Governance → Delivery → Outcomes → Learning → Strategy**

# Boundary Discipline

## Customer Outcomes System Owns

- gap identification  
- intervention framing  
- learning generation  
- routing recommendations  

## Customer Outcomes System Does Not Own

- executing interventions  
- prioritizing interventions  
- allocating resources  
- making governance decisions  
- redefining strategy directly  

---

## Strategy Execution System Owns

- refining strategic intent  
- adjusting outcomes and value hypotheses  
- updating strategic expectations  

---

## Product Delivery System Owns

- implementing execution changes  
- delivering product or operational improvements  

---

## Decision Intelligence System Owns

- improving measurement quality  
- enhancing signal visibility  
- refining metrics and instrumentation  

---

# Interface Logic

## Inputs to Intervention Playbook

- evaluated outcomes  
- identified gaps  
- interpreted signals  
- value realization assessments  
- confidence levels  

## Outputs from Playbook

- intervention category  
- root cause hypothesis  
- structured intervention definition  
- routed system ownership  

Outputs must remain **advisory and structured**, not controlling.

---

# Anti-Patterns Prevented by This Playbook

## 1. Jumping from Metrics to Action

Intervention requires evaluation first.

---

## 2. Treating All Gaps as Delivery Problems

Not all issues are execution issues — many are strategic or measurement-related.

---

## 3. Letting Outcomes Execute Work

The Outcomes system frames intervention but does not execute.

---

## 4. Skipping Root Cause Analysis

Interventions without hypotheses lead to ineffective changes.

---

## 5. Mixing System Ownership

Each intervention must have a clear owning system.

---

## 6. Reacting to Noise Instead of Evaluated Reality

Only validated learning should drive intervention.

---

# Why This Playbook Matters

This playbook matters because many organizations lack discipline in how they respond to outcomes.

Without a structured intervention model, teams often:

- overreact to individual metrics  
- apply fixes without understanding causes  
- misroute problems to the wrong teams  
- repeatedly address symptoms instead of root issues  
- weaken the integrity of the learning loop  

The **Outcome Intervention Playbook** ensures that response is:

- structured  
- evidence-based  
- system-aligned  
- repeatable  

It transforms outcome evaluation into effective organizational action.

# How to Use This Playbook

Use this playbook when:

- outcome evaluations identify gaps  
- teams need to determine appropriate response  
- distinguishing between strategy, delivery, and measurement issues  
- structuring intervention discussions in reviews  
- improving consistency of outcome-driven action  

It should be used alongside:

- Outcome Evaluation Model  
- Gap & Intervention Model  
- Outcome Signal Flow Diagram  
- Outcome to Strategy Feedback Diagram

# Relationship to the Broader Product Leadership Operating System

Within PLOS, this playbook operationalizes the transition from:

**Outcomes → Learning → System Response**

It ensures that learning does not remain conceptual, but is translated into structured intervention routed to the correct system.

This strengthens:

- delivery effectiveness  
- strategic refinement  
- measurement quality  
- overall system adaptability

# Summary

The **Outcome Intervention Playbook** defines the canonical method for converting evaluated outcome gaps into structured, system-aligned intervention.

It ensures that interventions are based on validated learning, routed to the correct system, and executed without violating architectural boundaries.

By preserving the separation between evaluation, decision, and execution, this playbook enables product organizations to respond to outcomes in a disciplined, effective, and scalable way.

Ultimately, it ensures that the **Product Leadership Operating System (PLOS)** operates as a **true learning system**, where evaluated outcomes consistently drive meaningful improvement across strategy, delivery, and measurement.
