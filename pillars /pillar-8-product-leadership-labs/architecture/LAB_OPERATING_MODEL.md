# Lab Operating Model

The **Lab Operating Model** defines how **Product Leadership Labs** operates as a controlled experimentation environment within the **Product Leadership Operating System (PLOS)**.

Where **PRODUCT_LEADERSHIP_LABS.md** defines what Labs is, this artifact defines how Labs is run in practice.

---

# Purpose

The purpose of the **Lab Operating Model** is to ensure that experimentation is:

- disciplined  
- bounded  
- reviewable  
- comparable  
- promotable only through formal governance  

It defines how experiments are proposed, structured, executed, evaluated, and either retired or promoted.

---

# Core Principle

> **Labs must enable exploration without destabilizing the canonical operating system**

Experiments may test new ideas, but they must remain isolated from production authority until validated and formally approved.

---

# Operating Lifecycle

Labs operates through a repeatable lifecycle.

## 1. Intake

An experiment begins with a clearly stated proposal that includes:

- the problem or opportunity being explored  
- the hypothesis being tested  
- the affected pillar or operating domain  
- expected learning value  
- proposed scope and duration  

No experiment should begin without explicit definition of intent and boundaries.

---

## 2. Framing

Before execution, the experiment must be framed within the PLOS architecture.

This includes:

- identifying affected systems  
- defining which boundaries must be preserved  
- confirming that the experiment does not create production authority  
- specifying how evidence, evaluation, and decisions will remain separated  

Framing ensures experiments remain architecturally safe before they begin.

---

## 3. Experiment Design

The experiment is then structured using the canonical template.

Design must define:

- inputs  
- scope  
- execution method  
- observable signals  
- review cadence  
- exit criteria  
- promotion possibility  

Design must also define how evaluation will be routed through the **Customer Outcomes System**.

---

## 4. Controlled Execution

Experiments are run in a bounded environment.

Execution must:

- remain within approved scope  
- avoid unauthorized production changes  
- preserve canonical mediation paths  
- document observations clearly  
- generate artifacts that can be reviewed later  

Labs is not a free-form innovation space. Execution must remain controlled and reviewable.

---

## 5. Evaluation

Experiment results must be evaluated through the **Customer Outcomes System**.

This means:

- signals remain evidence until interpreted  
- learning is generated through the canonical outcomes layer  
- experiment success or failure is not declared directly inside Labs without outcome evaluation discipline  

Labs may capture observations, but it does not own formal meaning-making.

---

## 6. Governance Review

After evaluation, governance determines whether the experiment:

- is discontinued  
- is iterated  
- is expanded  
- is promoted into canonical architecture  

Governance owns the promotion decision.

Labs may recommend continuation of exploration only through structured review inputs, not through direct authority.

---

## 7. Closure or Promotion

Every experiment must end in one of two states:

- **closed**  
- **promoted**  

Closed experiments remain as learning artifacts but do not affect canonical architecture.

Promoted experiments move into the appropriate production pillar through a formal transition process.

---

# Roles Within the Operating Model

## Labs
Owns:

- experiment structuring  
- controlled execution  
- observation capture  
- preparation of review artifacts  

## Customer Outcomes System
Owns:

- interpretation of experiment results  
- evaluation of outcome significance  
- validated learning  

## Governance
Owns:

- approval to continue, stop, scale, or promote  
- promotion decisions  

## Decision Intelligence System
Owns:

- signals, dashboards, metrics, and visibility used as experiment inputs  

## Affected Production Pillars
Own:

- formal adoption of promoted artifacts  
- production integration after promotion  

---

# Operating Rules

The Lab Operating Model must preserve the following rules:

- experiments must be explicitly scoped  
- experiments must have reviewable boundaries  
- experiments must not redefine pillar ownership  
- experiments must not bypass Customer Outcomes System  
- experiments must not convert raw signals directly into decisions  
- experiments must not become canonical by default  

Experiments must not introduce incremental production changes that accumulate into de facto canonical behavior without formal promotion.

All production impact must occur only after governance-approved promotion.

---

# Cadence

Labs should operate on a recurring review cadence sufficient to maintain control and visibility.

At minimum, every experiment should have:

- an intake review  
- at least one midpoint review for active experiments  
- a closure or promotion review  

The exact cadence may vary by experiment size, but recurring review is mandatory.

---

# Relationship to Other Pillars

The Lab Operating Model does not replace the **Product Leadership Operating Model**.

Instead, it provides the controlled experimental complement to the canonical operating system.

It supports system evolution without creating a parallel production model.

---

# Summary

The **Lab Operating Model** defines how experimentation is conducted inside **Product Leadership Labs**.

It ensures that experiments are:

- controlled  
- reviewable  
- bounded  
- evaluated through the correct systems  
- promoted only through formal governance  

This allows PLOS to evolve intentionally without sacrificing architectural integrity.
