# Product Leadership Playbooks

This repository defines **Pillar 7 — Product Leadership Playbooks** within the **Product Leadership Operating System (PLOS)**.

It provides the canonical set of **repeatable execution patterns** used to operationalize the system across **strategy, governance, delivery, and customer outcomes**.

Playbooks ensure that the operating system is not just designed — but **consistently and correctly executed**.

---

# Purpose

The purpose of the **Product Leadership Playbooks** is to:

- Standardize execution across the Product Leadership Operating System  
- Encode proven patterns for recurring product leadership scenarios  
- Reduce variability in how teams operate  
- Enable scalable, repeatable execution  
- Reinforce system integrity through consistent behavior  

Playbooks translate architecture into **practical, real-world execution**.

---

# Position Within the Operating System

The **Product Leadership Operating System (PLOS)** explains how organizations operate across the full loop:

> **Strategy → Governance → Delivery → Outcomes → Learning → Strategy**

The system is composed of eight pillars:

1. Product Leadership Systems Architecture (PLSA)  
2. Product Leadership Operating Model  
3. Portfolio Governance System  
4. Product Delivery System  
5. Customer Outcomes System  
6. Decision Intelligence System  
7. Product Leadership Playbooks *(this repository)*  
8. Product Leadership Labs  

---

# What Playbooks Are

Playbooks are:

- Structured, repeatable execution patterns  
- Scenario-based guides for how teams operate within PLOS  
- Mechanisms for consistent application of system behaviors  
- Cross-cutting enablers that operate across multiple systems  

They define:

> **How execution is carried out within the system — without redefining system responsibilities**

---

# What Playbooks Are NOT

Playbooks do NOT:

- Define strategy  
- Make governance decisions  
- Execute delivery work  
- Evaluate outcomes  
- Interpret data or signals  
- Replace system responsibilities  

They must not introduce new system logic or bypass existing systems.

---

# Core Principle

> **Playbooks define how to act — not what to decide, what to measure, or what is valuable**

Playbooks operate as the **execution pattern layer** of the Product Leadership Operating System.

They translate system design into consistent, repeatable action without introducing new system responsibilities or decision logic.

---

# System Boundaries (Non-Negotiable)

The integrity of the Product Leadership Operating System depends on strict separation of concerns:

- **Decision Intelligence (Pillar 6)** → signals, metrics, dashboards, visibility ONLY  
- **Customer Outcomes (Pillar 5)** → interpretation, evaluation, learning  
- **Governance (Pillar 3)** → decisions and prioritization  
- **Strategy (Pillar 1)** → direction and refinement  
- **Delivery (Pillar 4)** → execution  

### Critical Rule

> **Playbooks must NEVER collapse evidence, meaning, and decision-making into the same layer**

- Evidence = Decision Intelligence  
- Meaning = Customer Outcomes  
- Decisions = Strategy & Governance  

---

# Interface Discipline

All system interaction must follow the canonical mediation path:

> **Decision Intelligence → Customer Outcomes → Strategy / Governance**

Playbooks may participate in execution across this flow, but must NEVER:

- create direct DI → Governance or Strategy paths  
- bypass Customer Outcomes  
- reinterpret signals or outcomes

# Playbook Categories

Playbooks in this repository are organized by operating context so that execution patterns can be located, applied, and maintained consistently across the **Product Leadership Operating System (PLOS)**.

## Strategy Playbooks

Strategy playbooks define repeatable patterns for translating strategic direction into coordinated action.

They support activities such as:

- strategic planning preparation  
- strategic alignment  
- strategic reprioritization  
- strategy communication and operationalization  

These playbooks help teams act on strategy, but they do not define strategy themselves.

## Governance Playbooks

Governance playbooks define repeatable patterns for preparing, supporting, and operationalizing portfolio governance activities.

They support activities such as:

- portfolio review preparation  
- prioritization decision preparation  
- portfolio rebalance execution  
- governance follow-through  

These playbooks operate within the governance system, but they do not make governance decisions.

## Delivery Playbooks

Delivery playbooks define repeatable patterns for coordinating and sustaining execution across delivery teams.

They support activities such as:

- delivery plan alignment  
- cross-team execution coordination  
- release readiness  
- delivery risk response  

These playbooks improve execution consistency, but they do not replace delivery management or delivery governance.

## Outcomes Playbooks

Outcomes playbooks define repeatable patterns for preparing, structuring, and operationalizing outcome review and learning activities.

They support activities such as:

- outcome review preparation  
- learning synthesis  
- value realization follow-through  
- adoption and impact review support  

These playbooks structure how teams prepare for and participate in the **Customer Outcomes System**, but they do not interpret signals, evaluate outcomes, assign value, or generate learning.

## Cross-System Playbooks

Cross-system playbooks define repeatable execution patterns that span multiple systems across the PLOS loop.

They support activities such as:

- initiative lifecycle coordination  
- executive escalation handling  
- transformation execution  
- cross-functional operating alignment  

These playbooks are especially important where execution must move cleanly across strategy, governance, delivery, and outcomes without collapsing system boundaries.

# How to Use This Repository

This repository should be used as the canonical reference for how repeatable execution patterns are defined, structured, and applied within **PLOS**.

## 1. Start with the Architecture

Begin with the files in the **architecture/** directory.

These artifacts define what the **Product Leadership Playbooks** are, where they sit in the operating system, how they interact with other pillars, and what boundaries they must preserve.

This provides the architectural context required before authoring or using any playbook.

## 2. Apply the Standards

Use the files in the **standards/** directory before creating or editing playbooks.

These standards define:

- the canonical playbook template  
- naming conventions  
- authoring rules  
- review requirements  
- boundary guardrails  

No playbook should be created outside those standards.

## 3. Use Playbooks by Scenario

Use the **playbooks/** directory to locate the appropriate playbook for a recurring execution scenario.

Select the playbook based on the operating context:

- strategy  
- governance  
- delivery  
- outcomes  
- cross-system  

Each playbook should be used as a repeatable execution guide, not as a substitute for system ownership or decision authority.

## 4. Preserve System Boundaries During Use

When using a playbook, always verify that it remains inside its intended role.

A playbook may structure action, coordination, preparation, and follow-through.

A playbook may not:

- define strategy  
- make governance decisions  
- interpret data  
- evaluate outcomes  
- create new cross-system pathways  

All use of playbooks must preserve the canonical mediation path:

> **Decision Intelligence → Customer Outcomes → Strategy / Governance**

## 5. Review Before Adoption

Before a new playbook is considered canonical, it should be reviewed for:

- PLOS alignment  
- boundary integrity  
- cross-pillar consistency  
- terminology consistency  
- interface discipline  

Playbooks should strengthen the architecture, not create local exceptions to it.

# Why This Matters

The **Product Leadership Operating System** depends not only on clear architecture, but on consistent execution.

Without playbooks, organizations often experience the same recurring problems:

- execution quality varies by team or leader  
- governance decisions are implemented inconsistently  
- delivery coordination becomes personality-dependent  
- outcome review practices drift over time  
- operating maturity does not scale with organizational growth  

Playbooks address these problems by encoding repeatable execution patterns that can be used across teams and over time.

They make the operating system more durable by reducing ambiguity in how work is carried out.

They improve quality not by introducing new system logic, but by making correct system behavior easier to execute consistently.

This matters because an operating system that cannot be executed reliably will eventually degrade, no matter how well it is designed.

# Relationship to Other Pillars

The **Product Leadership Playbooks** repository exists to support the broader PLOS architecture, not to replace any other pillar.

## Relationship to Pillar 1 — Product Leadership Systems Architecture

The **Product Leadership Systems Architecture (PLSA)** defines the canonical system structure of the operating system.

Playbooks do not redefine that structure.

They operationalize behavior within it.

## Relationship to Pillar 2 — Product Leadership Operating Model

The **Operating Model** defines the leadership cadence, forums, and operating rhythm used to run the system.

Playbooks help teams execute consistently within that model.

They do not replace the operating model itself.

## Relationship to Pillar 3 — Portfolio Governance System

The **Portfolio Governance System** owns portfolio decisions, prioritization, sequencing, review, and rebalance.

Governance playbooks may support preparation, facilitation, and operationalization of those activities.

They do not make decisions or assume governance authority.

## Relationship to Pillar 4 — Product Delivery System

The **Product Delivery System** owns execution, coordination, readiness, and delivery flow.

Delivery playbooks support repeatable execution patterns within that system.

They do not replace delivery ownership or delivery control mechanisms.

## Relationship to Pillar 5 — Customer Outcomes System

The **Customer Outcomes System** owns interpretation, value evaluation, outcome judgment, and learning generation.

Outcomes-related playbooks may structure how teams prepare for or operate within that system.

They must never evaluate outcomes or generate learning themselves.

## Relationship to Pillar 6 — Decision Intelligence System

The **Decision Intelligence System** owns instrumentation, signals, metrics, dashboards, analytics, and visibility.

Playbooks may reference these outputs as inputs to execution.

They must never interpret those outputs, assign meaning to them, or use them to bypass **Customer Outcomes**.

## Relationship to Pillar 8 — Product Leadership Labs

**Product Leadership Labs** provide the experimentation, exploration, and developmental space for emerging methods, practices, and operating ideas.

Playbooks may eventually codify proven patterns that emerge from Labs.

Labs may inform playbook evolution, but experimental work must not be treated as canonical playbook architecture until it has been validated and formalized.

# Summary

The **Product Leadership Playbooks** repository defines the canonical set of repeatable execution patterns used to operationalize the **Product Leadership Operating System (PLOS)**.

Its purpose is to make execution more consistent, scalable, and durable across strategy, governance, delivery, and outcomes while preserving strict system boundaries.

Playbooks are not decision systems, interpretation systems, or analytics systems.

They are structured execution guides that help leaders and teams act correctly within the architecture already defined by PLOS.

When designed and governed correctly, playbooks strengthen the operating system by making the right way of working repeatable.
